Abdullah bin Hamad bin Isa Al Khalifa vs Michael Jackson (Start Date)

 

“Daily Express” (November 18, 2008)

The pop star, said to have taken £4.7million from Sheikh Abdulla Bin Hamad Bin Isa Al-Khalifa, now faces a multi-million-pound breach-of-contract action after, it is alleged, he failed to produce music as he he promised in a deal.

Jackson, who will give evidence from California via a video link, will argue that the money he took from the sheikh was made up of gifts.

The reclusive 50-year-old singer became close to Sheikh Abdullah in 2004 when the pair  planned to resurrect Jackson's music career, the High Court in London was told.

They had been introduced by Jackson’s brother Jermaine – who last year was in the UK on Celebrity Big Brother.

The sheikh had a “substantial interest in music,” had written songs and wanted the star to sing them, the court heard.

It is alleged Jackson signed a contract to produce music with his Arab friend who sent him some of his own songs.

Jackson was also said to have agreed to pen an autobiography and release charity singles for the victims of the Boxing Day tsunami and Hurricane Katrina.

Bankim Thanki QC, for the sheikh, told Mr Justice Sweeney that the day after the end of Jackson’s trial in California on child molestation charges, the star recorded a composition which the sheikh wanted released as a charity single for tsunami victims.

Mr Thanki promised the judge that a recording of the song would be played in court.

He said: “It shows the quality of Sheikh Abdullah's songwriting skills and that of Mr Jackson’s voice.”

The pair had a “close personal relationship” and discussed the chances of Jackson moving to Bahrain to “continue their musical collaboration in a more conducive environment.”

The sheikh had plans to revive Jackson’s career, releasing records on their own label.

It quickly became clear to the sheikh, however, that Jackson was in “very serious financial difficulties”.

The sheikh spent millions paying Jackson’s legal fees, moving him to Bahrain and supporting him, his family and his entourage.

The expenses included £250,000 for a European vacation for Jackson and his associates. Mr Thanki added: “The cost even included the expense of bringing out Mr Jackson’s hairdresser.

“It is not a conventional commercial dispute. Sheikh Abdulla began to support Jackson financially after 2005 when it became clear that Jackson was in serious financial difficulties, much to Sheikh Abdullah's surprise.”

The sheikh was first asked by a Jackson assistant for £25,000 to pay utility bills at Neverland.

The following month, April 2005, Jackson asked for a million dollars through an assistant.

Mr Thanki went on: “Sheikh Abdulla made many more payments on his behalf or to others, including Jackson’s $2.2million legal bill for his trial. He even paid for a lavish recording studio to be built so Jackson could develop the sheikh’s compositions.”

The sheikh is suing Jackson for allegedly reneging on a $7million (£4.7 million) agreement to repay money he advanced to the star during his worst financial troubles.

He claims he and Jackson entered into an agreement  committing the star to a recording contract, production of an autobiography and a musical stage play.

Sheikh Abdullah says he became Jackson’s “close friend” during the star’s six-month stay in Bahrain in 2005.

Jackson contests his claim, insisting there was no valid agreement and that the sheikh’s case is based on “mistake, misrepresentation and undue influence.”

The hearing is expected to last 12 days.

 

“Daily Mirror” (November 18, 2008)

Cash-strapped Michael Jackson was yesterday plunged into a bizarre legal battle with a mega-rich Arab prince who tried to help him relaunch his pop career.

Sheik Abdulla Al-Khalifa, 35, is suing the troubled star for £4.7million he claims he is owed after Jackson allegedly reneged on a record deal.

The tycoon – second son of the King of Bahrain – says he paid for a recording studio at the star’s Neverland ranch and settled his £1.4million lawyers’ bills over his criminal trial on child abuse charges.

He also handed over millions more for holidays, living expenses, security and generally “alleviating his financial plight”, the High Court was told.

The sheik’s lawyer, Mr Bankim Thanki QC, said Jackson, 50, begged Abdulla for money after being introduced to him by Jackson’s brother Jermaine in 2005 while they were staying in Bahrain.

Jackson claims the payments were gifts but Mr Thanki said: “The idea was to create a new recording label to kick start his musical career.”

The sheik even planned to write a song for Jackson which he hoped would be released as a charity single.

Mr Thanki added: “The idea was to relaunch his career from a base far away from his well publicised criminal trial which occupied the first half of 2005.

“He was under intense media scrutiny and against that background Bahrain was seen as a sanctuary from which he took very full advantage.”

In addition to the recording, he claimed Jackson also agreed to write an autobiography and a stage show.

In his defence, Jackson admits signing a document which he understood gave him a substantial shareholding in the 2 Seas recording company.

But he challenges the sheik’s description of him as “an experienced businessman” and says he never read the terms of the document and was never advised to take independent legal advice.

He claims the contract was entered into on the basis of “false representations” that the company was “capable of releasing and distributing, on a worldwide basis, recordings by an artist of international repute”.

Jackson also accuses the sheik of exerting “undue influence” over him at a time when he was emotionally exhausted. The case continues.

Daily Mirror (November 21, 2008)

Relations between Michael Jackson and the sheik who is suing him were already cool... but now things have turned icy.

Sheik Abdulla Al-Khalifa is suing Wacko for £4.7million after the oddball singer allegedly reneged on a record deal.

And the tycoon has gone to extraordinary lengths to itemise £350,000 of stuff which he claims he spent on the star - and that Wacko should have paid back.

And, chillingly, top of the list is a £3.79 tub of ICE CREAM.

Blimey, talk about milking it.

The second son of the King of Bahrain, 35, also wants to be compensated for £200 of Bodyshop lotions and products.

Well, you know what they say... look after the pennies.

There are other, more expensive, items on the list, such as a trip to a toy shop for MJ's family, a day in a waterpark, one Ferrari and two all-expenses-paid holidays in Dubai.

But we reckon it's the ice cream the sheik really wants. An insider told us: "It's the most bizarre list imaginable. Some bills are only for £5 - but the sheik wants every penny back.

"The sheik wants to be compensated for the strangest of things, including Michael's fave ice cream in the world, Haagen Daaz."

Michael, 50, who is said to be taking to the stand in the High Court in London himself on Monday, claims the items were gifts.

But the sheik's lawyer, Mr Bankim Thanki QC, said they were intended to kickstart his career after his criminal trial in 2005.

Wacko is now well on his way to releasing a comeback album, joining forces with the likes of top producers Will I Am, Akon, and Ne-Yo.

An insider said: "Michael will be in court on Monday and will be holding his head up high.

"He believed the sheik invited him to Bahrain as his guest so this is the last thing he expected.

"He is very hurt and baffled by these claims, but he is hoping his fans will support him."
