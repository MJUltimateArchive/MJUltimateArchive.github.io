"Rock with You"

Date range confirmed in Mike Smallcombe’s “Making Michael” (“Temperton was recording an album with his band in a New York studio in the spring of 1979 when he got a call from Quincy, who wanted him to write some funky disco tunes for Michael… [Temperton]: ‘They booked the studio in Los Angeles at the weekend when I wasn’t working, and I flew in over the weekend to cut the tracks.’... After cutting the rhythm tracks Temperton returned to New York, where he completed the lyrics, before flying back to Los Angeles the following weekend to record vocals with Michael. Background vocals were recorded on the Saturday afternoon, and the three lead vocals were done the next day… The three songs Temperton submitted for the album, ‘Off the Wall’, ‘Rock With You’ and ‘Burn This Disco Out’, are all disco themed.”)

 

Michael Jackson, “Hot Rocks: Michael Jackson” radio special (October 31, 1987) (archived)

Michael talks about how they came up with the song "Rock With You", and how it was written by former "Heatwave" member Rod Temperton… "Quincy told him to write a song about "The Rock", which is a dance.  But we wanted to have a double meaning about "rocking" a girl and doing the dance, the rock, you know, a little nasty (laughs)... and he came up with the song, which was wonderful."

 

Rod Temperton, songwriter, Chris Cadman’s “Michael Jackson the Maestro”

Rock With You was a rhythm section I’d had before.‘I thought I’d go with this one because he’s good at handling very melodic things.

 

John "JR" Robinson, drummer, “Rhythm” magazine (July 2010)

..."Rod [Temperton] was this great writer. All of us converged in LA at the exact same time and all through Quincy, which was extraordinary", says JR. "About a week or so later, Quincy asked me, 'Why don't we get your band [Rufus] and record this next tune?' That happened to be 'Rock with You', so it was Bobby Watson on bass, who is in my band now, and Hawk Wolinski, the keyboard player who wrote 'Ain't Nobody'. So, it was Rufus that cut 'Rock with You', Michael's greatest song, in my opinion."

All this time later, JR still singles out "Rock With You" as his favorite song from his work with Michael, even though he was under pressure in the studio at the time. With Rod Temperton in the room, JR was put on the spot to come up with a truly memorable opening for the track.

"Quincy looks at me and he goes, 'JR, I want you to come up with a drum fill that will be forever associated with his song.' I look at him and go, 'Wow, you're not going to put me under any pressure, are you?' Rod had a demo, so I took an idea from that and came up with that drum fill. Generally, as a drummer, when I'm playing in some sort of 16th-note vibe, I don't like any triplets going with 16th-notes in a po tune, it just sounds foreign to me. So, what did I do? I did exactly like that, I combined a triplet with a 16th note accent on the kick drum and came up with the intro. You could ask any lay person in the world, when they hear that, they don't even have to hear the song, they know it's 'Rock With You'".

 

Bruce Swedien, studio engineer, “Goldmine” magazine (April 9, 2010) (archived)

And if you think of ‘Rock With You’ and the harmonies, holy cow … and the background. Michael sang every one of those harmonies. Every one. There’s no other voice on those recordings.”

“Sound on Sound” (November 2009) (archived)

The 'Billie Jean' drum sound, like that on many of Swedien's records, also relied on a purpose‑built drum riser the engineer originally had constructed for recording the previous album's 'Rock With You': a braced, eight‑foot-square wooden platform raised 10 inches off the ground. "The reason I wanted the drums to be up off the floor,” he wrote in his autobiography, Make Mine Music, "is to keep the low‑frequency drum sounds (such as the bass drum and tom‑toms) from coupling with the surface of the floor and entering the sound pickup area of the microphones on the other instruments in the session. This secondary pickup of the low‑frequency end of the drum kit can travel through walls! By putting the drum setup on my drum platform, those low sounds never had the chance of connecting with the floor and creating off‑mic, obscure, second‑hand pickup.”

'Rock With You' is also an excellent showcase for another of Swedien's creative live‑room production techniques. Each of the backing-vocal lines was first double‑tracked with a close mic, then Jackson moved a couple of steps back from the mic for another pass, while Swedien increased the preamp gain to match his level with the previous takes. Finally, an even more distant pass was captured using a Blumlein stereo pair, again matched for level. The result: an increased density of early reflections, which creates a natural depth and width to the soundfield.

 

Chris Cadman, author, “Michael Jackson the Maestro”

Re-arranged version, slightly jazzed-up (jazzy guitar and added handclaps), dating from early 1980, was included on new pressings of OFF THE WALL, replacing the original recording. Original version featured on the original CD pressing issued in Japan, and on some later Japanese pressings.

Masters At Work Remix and Frankie’s remixes featured unreleased vocals and ad-libs not heard on the album version, as the remixers were given access to the master tapes of the song, which included additional vocals.
