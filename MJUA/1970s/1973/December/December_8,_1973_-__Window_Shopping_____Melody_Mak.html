“Melody Maker” magazine (January 12, 1974) (archived)

MoWest Studios, the private recording facilities for Motown artists, are located in the heart of Hollywood, directly across the street from a high school's baseball diamond.

But penetrating its environs rivals getting into the White House, pre-Watergate days.

Not only is one forced to sign in and out, but furthermore all doors only open to the touch of mysteriously hidden buttons, carefully disguised to appear solely to the proper person, A couple of large bouncers sit in the ante-room, making the enclosed area fittingly forbidden.

Once inside the private doors, however, the recording complex becomes inviting. Situated around a patio, the walls are splashed with bright colors and healthy plants adorn table tops.

This afternoon, one of the studios is occupied by The Jackson Five, hard at work on their next super album.

Jermaine, one week shy of marriage, is in the soundbooth, laying down the lead vocal for a song called "Window Shopping", while his brothers, ensconced within the producer's box, work out their all important counterpoint, singing along without benefit of microphone or tape.

There are quite a few takes as the producer works with Jermaine for emphasis.

Finally, Jackie pipes in with "he was sharper before" and the producer calls into the microphone "one more for Jackie".

It's perfect and the Jackson brothers descend into the recording booth to lay down the backup track.

It's a heady time for the Jacksons (although recently you'd be hard pressed to find a time that wasn't); not only the wedding, but they are already preparing for their first tour of Nigeria.

...During a break, one by one they go out to the patio for a little fresh air and some conversation.

Whenever more than one Jackson brother is together at the same time they get into routines, trying to answer questions without cracking up with "in" jokes but rarely succeeding.

Michael, for instance, did a soft shoe number while talking about his writing.

"I spend a lot of time with my songs", he says, sort of mastering the sly art of shyness. I want to make them perfect, really good.

"Each time I write a song, I think to myself that this has to be a top ten record.

"I have more trouble with the lyrics than the music", he adds, "so I write a lot with Jermaine."

With the unfailing precision of someone who has been turning out million selling records since he was 8 years old, Michael sums up the Midas touch by saying "all you need for a hit record is the right kind of feeling."

Just what that right kind of feeling is might be left uninterpreted.

Their recent tour of New Zealand, Australia, and Japan left little doubt that they're superstars across the world.

..."We watched our cartoon in Japan", they tell you, marveling at seeing their animated selves speak a foreign language so fluently.

"But Australia was pretty old fashioned", they added. "There weren't many color televisions."

...Four years ago, the family built an 8 track recording studio in their backyard, just a stone's throw from the all important basketball court.

Just recently they completed the finishing touches on a 24 track studio so that the musical family need never venture past their own doorsteps again.

"We're recording here at MoWest now, but in the future we plan to record almost everything at home. The thing is at home there'll be no pressure, you can take things easy and you can work anytime during the 24 hours."

...In the early part of January, The Jackson Five will make their third appearance on the Sonny and Cher Comedy Hour.

On the show, they'll do [illegible] sketches as well as introduce their next single, a concept that originated way back in the old Ed Sullivan Show.

"We've kinda made a tradition out of breaking songs on television", Jackie tells you. "You get to more people that way, it seems a good alternative to test markets.

"Unlike a concert, people can really see what you're doing too."

Tomorrow (Friday) they'll tape a show called "One More Time" which [illegible] The Jackson Five singing with the likes of Pat Boone and the legendary Mills Brothers.

"Television is good for us", they say, "because it lets you deal more closely with an audience. They can really see you, but Michael gets scared."

The 13-year-old superstar looks beguilingly shy and adds under his breath, "I'm not scared."

When asked why none of the three Jackson sisters ever became part of the singing group, there's a long pause.

Finally, Michael shrugs and said, "they never wanted to be in the group. Besides, if we let Janet in the group, she'd have eaten up all the [illegible]."

But at least one of the sisters will be following in her brothers' footsteps. Seventeen-year-old La Toya Jackson is about to record a song especially written for her by Michael and Jermaine. Michael, additionally, is planning to produce the single.

"I want to get into other things", he says dancing around. "I just cut some tracks with Glen Campbell" and undoubtedly the Motown film organization is looking out for the project that will put Michael across on the big screen as they have on records, in concerts and on television.

Back in the recording studio, standing up under an enormous poster that reads "Diana Ross is Billie Holiday", Jermaine is singing out what will certainly be another million selling single...
